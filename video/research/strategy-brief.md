### Pain Point Section (PAS)
Job: Make the visitor feel understood before showing a single piece of work.

Format: Short PAS with scannable checkmarks.

**Problem — "Does this sound familiar?"**
- ✓ Your product launched, but users keep dropping off at the same point
- ✓ Developers are building features, but nobody's talking about how they actually feel to use
- ✓ You've had design work done before — and it looked good in the presentation, but confused users in the real world
- ✓ You know something's off with the experience, but can't pinpoint exactly what
- ✓ Your team is moving fast and design is always the last thing considered

**Agitate:**
Every week without a clear UX direction is a week of development time building the wrong thing. **Features ship. Users still churn.** The product looks finished. It just isn't enjoyable to use.

You've probably tried patching it — a new onboarding flow here, a redesigned button there. But piecemeal fixes don't fix broken interaction logic. And your users feel every one of those rough edges.

**Solve:**
You don't need a bigger team or a six-month agency retainer. You need a designer who starts with the problem, not the pixels — and works with your team from research through to the final interaction detail.

### Process / How I Work
Job: Reduce anxiety about what it's like to work with Anandu. Show the path from "we have a problem" to "this is solved."

3–4 steps, simple language, no jargon:

1. **Discovery** — We start with a conversation. I want to understand your users, your business, and what's not working before I touch a single screen.
2. **Research & Define** — I map the problem. User flows, pain points, interaction gaps — all made visible before any design starts.
3. **Design & Iterate** — Wireframes, prototypes, interaction details. You see the work in progress, not just the final version.
4. **Handoff & Support** — Clean design files, specs, and I stay involved through development to make sure what gets built matches what was designed.

The page must feel like it was designed to convert, not to win a Dribbble like. Restraint and clarity beat visual complexity here.
